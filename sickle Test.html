Why your doctor may request a Sickling Test?

Image by rawpixel.com on Freepik

Your doctor may request a sickling test if they suspect you have sickle cell disease or trait based on your symptoms, family history, or ethnic background. Sickle cell disease is most commonly found in people of African, Caribbean, Middle Eastern, Eastern Mediterranean, and Indian descent.

The sickling test is a simple and relatively inexpensive way to screen for sickle cell disease or trait. If the test is positive, further testing may be done to confirm the diagnosis and determine the type of sickle cell disease present.

It is important to note that sickle cell trait is usually not a cause for concern, but individuals with sickle cell disease may experience complications such as pain, organ damage, and infections. Early detection and management of sickle cell disease can improve outcomes and quality of life for those affected.

Sickling test is a screening test done to screen one's blood for sickle cell disease. Sickle cell disease is a term used to describe the inheritance of haemoglobin S with any other haemoglobin. This test cannot differentiate between sickle cell disease and sickle cell trait. The main forms of sickle cell disease are:

  • Homozygous sickle cell anaemia (HbSS): where the individual inherits haemoglobin S genes from both parents.
  • Sickle cell haemoglobin D (HbSD): where the individual inherits haemoglobin S gene with haemoglobin D gene.
  • Sickle cell haemoglobin C (HbSC): where the individual inherits haemoglobin S gene with haemoglobin C gene.
  • Sickle Cell Beta thalassemia: where the individual inherits haemoglobin S gene with one of the beta thalassemia genes. Clinically, it is severe and life-threatening.

A normal red blood cell containing normal haemoglobin will appear biconcave in shape and transverse tiny blood vessels even under an oxygen-deprived environment. Inheriting haemoglobin S gene causes your red blood cells to sickle (crescent) in shape under deoxygenated conditions. These red blood cells are unable to meander through blood vessels to transport oxygen to all parts of the body, resulting in a crisis.

The principle of the sickling test is to be able to see the reaction going on in your body (in vivo), the laboratory scientist demonstrates this reaction intro. Blood is mixed with a chemical reducing agent such as sodium metabisulphite (di-sodium disulphite) on the slide. The mixture is covered with a cover slip and incubated at room temperature for 45-60 minutes. The reducing agent deoxygenates the haemoglobin in the red cells providing the conditions for cells containing HbS to sickle. This is then visualized by the microscope.

Useful Statistics on Sickle Cell

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), sickle cell disease affects millions of people worldwide, with an estimated 300,000 babies born with the disease each year.

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) reports that sickle cell disease is most prevalent in sub-Saharan Africa, where an estimated 75% of sickle cell disease cases occur.

In the United States, sickle cell disease affects approximately 100,000 people, primarily those of African descent. It is also found in people of Hispanic, Middle Eastern, and Mediterranean descent. The CDC provides this statistic as well.

The American Society of Hematology states that about 1 in 13 African Americans carries the sickle cell trait.

The American Society of Hematology also reports that sickle cell disease can lead to a shortened lifespan, with the average life expectancy for a person with sickle cell disease being in the mid-40s for men and late 40s for women.

Finally, the CDC notes that sickle cell disease can cause a variety of complications, including anemia, acute pain episodes (also called sickle cell crises), infections, organ damage, stroke, and pulmonary hypertension.
