Understanding Your Genotype

Written by SUSSAN AKORFA || Published on 22 April, 2023.



Have you ever considered finding out your genotype? Or do you believe it is non-infectious, and therefore not necessary? Your genotype can provide important information about your health and potential risks for certain diseases. The term "genotype" comes from the word "gene" (pronounced "jeen") and was coined by Danish botanist Wilhelm Johannsen in 1909.

What does Genotype mean?

Image by kjpargeter on Freepik
Genotype refers to an individual's genetic makeup inherited from their parents. This genetic makeup is the unique sequence of Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) present in the individual. The DNA sequence of an individual's genotype contains information that directs the development, growth, and function of their cells, tissues, and organs throughout their life.



What determines an organism's Genotype?

An individual's genotype is determined by the genetic material inherited from their parents - one from the father and another from the mother. Corresponding hemoglobin is produced in the red blood cells to enable the transport of oxygen throughout the body. This hemoglobin contains two alpha globins and two beta globins linked together by an iron molecule.
The alpha globins contain 141 amino acid sequences, and the beta globins contain 146 amino acid chains, forming the normal hemoglobin A (Hemoglobin A=98%, Hemoglobin A2=2%, Hemoglobin S=0%). Genetic abnormalities in the synthesis of the amino acid chains can lead to the production of abnormal hemoglobins S, C, D, E, and thalassemia syndromes.

What are the types of Genotypes present in humans?

The genotypes in humans are AA, AS, AC, SS, SC, and AF in neonates, which diminish as an individual ages.

Why is it important to know your genotype?

Knowing your genotype can help you make informed decisions about your health and the health of your future children. Inheriting two abnormal hemoglobins from both parents can be fatal and a peril to a healthy life, emphasizing the need to be compatible with one partner. Inheriting hemoglobin S with any other abnormal hemoglobin is termed sickle cell disease. Inheriting hemoglobin SS from both parents is termed sickle cell anemia.

By understanding your genotype and potential genetic risks, you can take steps to prevent or manage health conditions, such as seeking genetic counseling, getting regular check-ups and screenings, and making lifestyle changes. Additionally, having knowledge of your genotype can help you make informed decisions about family planning and reproductive options.

How does sickle cell anemia occur?

Sickle cell anemia occurs when two abnormal hemoglobin S are inherited from both parents. Hemoglobin S is produced by replacing glutamic acid with valine at position six of the beta chain. Hemoglobin S is less soluble under oxygen-deprived conditions and tends to polymerize and form sickle shapes. This causes red blood cells containing such hemoglobin to be unable to travel through small capillaries and veins, resulting in low or no oxygen circulation to all parts of the body. Consequently, the individual experiences shortness of breath known as a crisis.

Additional information to consider:

  • Genotype testing is important not only for reproductive planning but also for identifying genetic disorders that may affect an individual's health.
  • In addition to sickle cell disease, there are other genetic disorders that can be identified through genotype testing, such as cystic fibrosis, Tay-Sachs disease, and Huntington's disease.
  • There are different methods of genotype testing, including blood tests, saliva tests, and buccal swab tests.
  • In some countries, genotype testing is mandatory for certain populations, such as couples planning to get married or have children.
  • Genetic counseling can help individuals and couples understand their genotype results, assess their risks, and make informed decisions about their reproductive options.
  • Advances in genetic research and technology are leading to new discoveries and applications of genotype testing, such as personalized medicine and gene editing.
